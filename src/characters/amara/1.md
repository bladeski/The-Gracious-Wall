---
title: April 17th, Year 90
---
Today was my 21st birthday.
Legend has it this used to be a time of celebration — a moment when a person stepped into adulthood and became a responsible member of society. But for us, that happened at fifteen. Not the celebration, exactly, but the responsibility, the expectations, and the obligation to contribute to the community. In that sense, I feel like I’ve been an adult for a long time now, and birthdays are just a countdown to the “big 25.” I’d rather not think about that.

I’m not sure anyone even remembered my birthday, aside from Marek — who greeted me this morning with a massive grin and a tight squeeze when I entered the gardens — and Iain, who came all the way up from the archive to give me this journal. I’m not sure what I’m supposed to do with it, but he said perhaps it would help me find my purpose. I’m grateful, and I hope he’s right, though I don’t know what difference it will make. Besides, all he ever does at the archive is read other people’s stories, so maybe he just wants access to my innermost thoughts and secrets when I’m gone. Not that I have any.

The rest of the day was much the same as yesterday, and the days before that. Garden work. I cleared and hoed last year’s lettuce patch, prepping it for carrot seeds, and spent hours wrestling with the old irrigation pump before summer really sets in. Charlie kept pestering me to help him — he’s late planting the potatoes — but the pump was being its usual difficult self and refused to start.

I do like working in the garden. It’s peaceful, and there’s something deeply satisfying about it. Marek sat with me at lunch and talked at length about the best way to prune rose bushes. We don’t grow roses — vegetables only — but listening to him describe the care and attentiveness he shows his own garden was strangely comforting today. He knows so much about plants, and I always enjoy talking to him.

As an elder, though, he can’t help pushing me to find a mate. Today was no exception. “I hate seeing the way people look at you,” is his usual line, but today he pressed harder. “You really should find someone before it’s too late. How else will you have a chance of making it onto the council?” I know he cares and wants what’s best for me, and I appreciate that he’s not like those who look down their noses at me for “avoiding my duty.” Still, I’m not sure I want to be a town-surrogate — or a council member at all. Maybe he’s right. I just don’t feel like that’s why I’m here.

It’s late now. The sun set hours ago and the ’syth is dark and quiet. I can hear Kess snoring in the next room, and who knows what the others are doing.

Goodnight, journal.
