---
title: April 21st, Year 90
---
Iain came by the gardens today, mostly to say hello, but also to check how I’ve been getting on with the journal. I had to admit — somewhat sheepishly — that I’ve only managed the one entry so far. I expected him to be disappointed, but he seemed genuinely pleased that I’d at least started. He said that was what mattered.

Things got a little strange after that. Charlie turned up, and the atmosphere thickened almost immediately — so much so you could have cut it with a knife. There’s clearly something going on between them. After a few uncomfortable moments, Charlie said, “Don’t you have some carrots to sow, Amara?” It wasn’t really a question, and it was rich coming from him of all people. Iain didn’t linger after that and headed straight back to the archive. When I asked Charlie about it later, he brushed it off and claimed there was nothing at all, which only made it feel more like something.

I wrapped up my work in the gardens just after lunch, having finished sowing the last of the carrot seeds and tidied the beds, then made my way down to the yard at the bottom of the hill. That’s where we salvage and strip old machines and try to make something useful from what’s left. Most of us in the ’syth have two or more functions, and I’m both gardener and scrapper. They’re equally essential to keeping the place running, but it’s the yard that really makes me feel alive.

There’s something about finding, fixing, or salvaging parts to give them new purpose that just works for me. I can lose hours there, listening to the machines, tweaking and tuning, coaxing old beasts back into life — or cobbling together new ones to make someone else’s work a little easier.

I had some extra time today, so I pulled out the old radio I’ve been tinkering with. Callen says it’s a waste of effort, that the radio network’s been dead for decades. Still, I can’t help wondering what might happen if I actually got it working. What if we could talk to the outside world again?

We trade with the nearby settlements, and we head to the Nauld for the summer turnin’, but beyond that there are only rumours and half-believed stories from the occasional traveller — most of which are treated with deep suspicion. To bring the network back, to truly know what lies beyond the wall at the edge of town, could open up opportunities none of us can even imagine. Of course, I can already hear the elders warning us about all the dangers it would bring.

I think I’ll go and see Iain in the morning. He’s always full of ideas, and he never judges. Maybe he’ll even tell me what’s really going on with Charlie.

Goodnight, journal.
